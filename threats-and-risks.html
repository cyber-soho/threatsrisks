Hour 1 — Introduction to Threats and Risks

Definitions: Threat vs Risk

Threat: A potential cause of an unwanted incident that may harm systems or data. Example: a hacker trying to access a hospital’s patient database.

Risk: The likelihood that a threat will exploit a vulnerability and cause harm. Example: if a hospital doesn’t patch outdated software, the risk of a hacker succeeding is high.

Categories of Threats

Internal threats

Originating from inside the organization. Example: a disgruntled employee copying sensitive files to a USB stick.

External threats

Coming from outside the organization. Example: phishing emails targeting employee logins.

Natural threats

Environmental hazards. Example: a flood damaging a data‑center server room.

Types of Cyber Threats

Malware

Viruses, worms, ransomware. Example: ransomware encrypting company data and demanding payment.

Phishing & Social Engineering

Example: a fake email pretending to be from the CEO asking for a bank transfer.

Denial‑of‑Service (DoS)

Example: overloading a small business website until it crashes.

Advanced Persistent Threats (APT)

Example: a state‑sponsored group infiltrating a government agency for months.

Vulnerabilities & Exploits

Vulnerability

A weakness in a system. Example: default passwords on routers.

Exploit

A method used to take advantage of a vulnerability. Example: logging in with default credentials.

Threat Actors

Script Kiddies

Use existing tools with little skill. Example: a teen runs free tools against school computers.

Hacktivists

Politically or socially motivated attackers.

Organized Crime

Financially motivated, structured groups.

Nation States

State‑sponsored, persistent capabilities.

Hour 2 — Overview of Information Assets

What are Information Assets?

Any data, device, or component supporting information‑related activities. Example: an employee’s laptop with HR access is an information asset.

Types of Information Assets

Hardware

Servers, laptops, routers. Example: a firewall appliance in a data center.

Software

Applications & operating systems. Example: Microsoft Office for daily operations.

Data

Structured/unstructured (e.g., customer records). Example: a spreadsheet with salaries.

Personnel

People who use/manage assets. Example: an IT admin managing accounts.

Procedures & Policies

Example: a disaster recovery plan ensuring continuity.

Why are Assets Valuable?

Support business processes

Example: a CRM managing customer relations/sales.

Ensure compliance

Example: financial data stored for audits under regulations.

Protect reputation

Example: a university safeguarding student grades.

Asset Classification Levels
  • Public
  • Internal Use
  • Confidential
  • Highly Confidential

Examples: marketing brochures are public; payroll data is highly confidential.

Threats by Asset Type

Data leak in cloud storage

Example: a misconfigured cloud bucket exposing private data.

Physical theft

Example: a lost smartphone with corporate email access.

Hour 3 — Identifying Assets & Permissions

Identify Information Assets
  • Inventory hardware & software (e.g., log all laptops and installed software)
  • Document data repositories (e.g., list all company databases)
  • Map where sensitive data is stored/transmitted (e.g., email attachments on unsecured networks)
Role of Access Controls

Permissions

Determine who may view, edit, or delete data. Example: HR can see salaries; regular staff cannot.

Least Privilege

Example: a receptionist should not have payroll access.

Access Control Models

DAC

User decides file permissions (e.g., Windows file sharing).

MAC

System‑enforced labels (e.g., classified government systems).

RBAC

Roles map to permissions (e.g., doctors vs admin staff in a hospital).

Managing Permissions — Tools

Active Directory

Centralized identity & access.

LDAP

Directory protocol integration.

File System ACLs

Fine‑grained filesystem permissions.

Risks from Misconfiguration

Over‑permissioning

Example: an intern accidentally deletes a critical DB table.

Privilege escalation

Example: a user exploits a flaw to gain admin rights.

Key Definitions & Relationships

TermDefinitionExample
ThreatA potential cause of harm to an asset or system.A hacker attempting to break into a server.
VulnerabilityA weakness or flaw that can be exploited by a threat.Outdated software with an unpatched hole.
RiskLikelihood a threat exploits a vulnerability, causing loss.High risk if critical systems are outdated and exposed.
ImpactThe extent of damage or consequences if risk materializes.Financial loss, downtime, reputational damage.

Formula (conceptual): Risk ≈ Threat × Vulnerability × Impact. If any is effectively zero (e.g., no vulnerability), overall risk is minimized.

Active or passive?Often external & activePassive flaw/weaknessCalculated scenarioMeasured result

Analogy: burglar nearby (threat), broken lock (vulnerability), chance of break‑in (risk), value of stolen items (impact).

Malware & Notable Incidents — Case Studies

Viruses — Mydoom (2004)

One of the fastest‑spreading email worms; caused massive damages via DoS and backdoors.

Learn more: Mydoom — Wikipedia

Worms — ILOVEYOU (2000)

Spread via email “I LOVE YOU,” infected millions, overwrote files and mailed itself to contacts.

Learn more: ILOVEYOU — Wikipedia

Ransomware — WannaCry (2017)

Exploited a Windows flaw, encrypted data, demanded Bitcoin; impacted 200k+ computers in 150 countries, including the UK NHS.

Learn more: WannaCry — Wikipedia

Phishing/Social Engineering — DNC Hack (2016)

Spear‑phishing led to network compromise and major email leaks with political repercussions.

Learn more: DNC attacks — Wikipedia

Denial‑of‑Service — “Mafiaboy” (2000)

Canadian teen launched DoS waves crippling major sites (Yahoo!, eBay, CNN), exposing internet fragility.

Learn more: WIRED (2012)

Advanced Persistent Threat — Gamaredon

Russian state‑linked group persistently targeting Ukrainian entities since 2013 with evolving campaigns.

Learn more: MITRE ATT&CK — G0047

Zero‑Day Exploits — Stuxnet (2010)

Highly sophisticated worm exploiting multiple zero‑days to physically damage nuclear centrifuges.

Learn more: Stuxnet — Wikipedia
