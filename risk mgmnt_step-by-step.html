Lecture: Cybersecurity Risk Management

Course: College Attestation Program of Cybersecurity - Prevention and Intervention

Objective: To provide a foundational understanding of what cybersecurity risk is, how it's managed using structured frameworks, and how to develop effective mitigation strategies for novice college students.

Part 1: Introduction to Risk Management (15 Minutes)

Instructor Notes

Start by defining a Risk Management Framework (RMF). Use the "secure house blueprint" analogy to make it relatable. Explain that frameworks provide a consistent, structured way to manage risks, ensuring no major threats are overlooked. This helps organizations make smart decisions about where to spend money on security and comply with laws.

Guiding Questions

The main purpose is to have a structured plan. It makes sure you don't miss anything important and that everyone in the company is on the same page. Just buying antivirus software ignores other risks, like employees falling for phishing scams or a fire in the server room.

They might waste money on the wrong things, different departments might focus on different risks while leaving huge gaps elsewhere, and they might fail to protect their most important data, leading to a major breach.

Part 2: Exploring Key Frameworks (40 Minutes)

NIST RMF (National Institute of Standards and Technology)

A very detailed, step-by-step "recipe" used heavily by the U.S. government. It provides a comprehensive and repeatable process for managing cybersecurity risks.

Learn more on the official NIST website →

ISO 31000 (International Organization for Standardization)

A general, high-level guide for managing any kind of risk (not just cybersecurity). It's used by multinational corporations and focuses on principles and processes rather than specific controls. A key principle is "continual improvement."

Learn more on the official ISO website →

FAIR (Factor Analysis of Information Risk)

The "financial" framework. Its goal is to put a dollar value on risk, helping to justify security spending to executives in business terms.

ALE (Annualized Loss Expectancy) = LEF (Loss Event Frequency) x PLM (Probable Loss Magnitude)

Risk = How often it happens x How much it costs

Learn more on the official FAIR Institute website →

Guiding Questions

High impact. A breach of patient records would have severe consequences for patient privacy (confidentiality), could lead to incorrect medical treatment if data is changed (integrity), and would be a disaster if doctors couldn't access it (availability).

They might manage risks like pirate attacks, fuel price spikes, port closures due to weather, or supply chain disruptions.

It allows them to make a financial case. They can argue that spending $50,000 is worthwhile if it can significantly reduce that $200,000 annual risk, showing a clear return on investment (ROI).

Part 3: The Risk Management Process in Action (45 Minutes)

Risk Treatment Options

After identifying and analyzing a risk, you must decide how to respond. There are four main strategies.

Accept

Do nothing. Acknowledge the risk, but the cost to fix outweighs the potential damage.

Avoid

Eliminate the risk entirely by stopping the activity that causes it (e.g., shutting down a server).

Mitigate

Reduce the risk. Implement controls to lower its likelihood or impact. (Most common)

Transfer

Share the risk. Shift the financial burden to a third party, like through cyber insurance.

Interactive Scenario

"Our small e-commerce company has an old, unpatched web server running a non-essential marketing blog. There's a high likelihood it could be hacked, but the impact would be low (no customer data). The cost to fix is $15,000. What should we do?"

Guiding Questions

I could run a vulnerability scan on the college website, organize a brainstorming session with the IT staff, or review historical data from other colleges that have been attacked.

Accept: A good choice because the impact is low and the cost to fix is high. The company can officially acknowledge the risk and spend its limited funds on protecting more critical systems.

Avoid: Also a great choice. If the blog isn't essential to the business, simply turning off the server eliminates the risk entirely for free.

Part 5: Conclusion & Q&A (5 Minutes)

Key Takeaways

  • Risk management is a continuous, structured process.
  • Frameworks like NIST, ISO 31000, and FAIR provide that structure.
  • The process involves identifying, analyzing, evaluating, and treating risks.
  • Treatment isn't just about fixing things; it can also mean accepting, avoiding, or transferring risk.
